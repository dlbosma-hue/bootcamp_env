## 1. Company Overview

National Public Radio (NPR) is an American public broadcasting organisation headquartered in Washington, D.C., operating as a national syndicator to a network of over 1,000 public radio stations across the United States. As a non-profit, member-supported broadcaster, NPR occupies a distinctive position in the American media landscape — publicly funded yet editorially independent, with a stated commitment to public service journalism. Its wide reach and trusted reputation make its editorial choices on representation and inclusion particularly consequential for how marginalised communities are reflected in mainstream discourse.

## 5. Language and Framing

The automated RSS analysis flagged **no overtly problematic language** in the 10 headlines and teasers reviewed. No slurs, outdated terminology, or explicitly harmful framing was detected at the surface level.

That said, the framing of Latino communities as a political "test" case — a recurring motif across two of the three relevant headlines — warrants scrutiny against best-practice standards. Media Diversity Watch benchmarks, drawing on trauma-informed and community-centred journalism guidance, recommend that coverage of minority communities should not be confined to moments of political utility or crisis. On language more broadly: inclusive language guidelines in the knowledge base emphasise people-first disability language, the use of correct names and pronouns in LGBTQ+ coverage, and avoidance of gendered exceptionalism (e.g., "female doctor" constructions). ## 10. Recommendations

1. **Commission a longitudinal full-text audit (NPR Editorial Standards team, within 3 months):** The current RSS-only methodology captures surface signals only. NPR should provide or facilitate access to full article text across a minimum 30-day period to enable robust assessment of sourcing diversity, language use, and community portrayal. Media Diversity Watch should scope and initiate this extended audit by Q2 2026.

2. **Conduct an internal byline demographic audit (NPR People & Culture / Editorial Leadership, within 6 months):** NPR should audit the gender, racial, and disability identity composition of contributing authors across all platforms and publish findings. This would allow external researchers to move beyond surface-level byline counts and assess whether workforce diversity is translating into editorial representation. A public-facing annual disclosure model — as adopted by several UK broadcasters — is recommended as best practice.

3. **Develop and publish a community coverage framework for underrepresented groups (NPR Editorial Standards, within 6 months):** NPR should establish explicit editorial targets for coverage frequency of women, LGBTQ+ people, disabled people, and racial and ethnic minority communities beyond political event cycles. This framework should include guidance on avoiding political-utility framing and centring community voices as experts and agents. The framework should be reviewed quarterly against actual output.

4. **Integrate inclusive sourcing tracking into editorial workflow (NPR Editorial team, ongoing from Q3 2026):** Drawing on established models such as the BBC's 50:50 Project, NPR should implement a sourcing diversity tracker requiring journalists to record the gender, race, and disability status of quoted sources per article. Aggregated data should be reviewed at team level monthly and reported publicly on an annual basis, allowing external accountability on the sourcing diversity gap that this audit was unable to assess.
