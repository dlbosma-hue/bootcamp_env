## 1. Company Overview

## 3. Portrayal Within Content

Where marginalised communities do appear in The Guardian's coverage, the framing tends toward victimisation rather than agency, leadership, or recovery. The article on two women arrested in Uganda for allegedly kissing in public — while reporting on a genuine human rights concern — frames its subjects primarily through the lens of legal jeopardy and criminal vulnerability, with limited contextualisation of LGBTQ+ activism or community resilience in Uganda. Similarly, the article on Australia's gender pay gap, headlined *"Men almost twice as likely as women to earn high salaries in 'reality check' on Australia's gender pay gap,"* frames the issue statistically and structurally, which is appropriate, but the sample contains no corresponding articles that centre women as economic leaders or policy drivers. Trauma-informed journalism best practice, as documented in the Media Diversity Watch knowledge base, recommends that outlets balance harm-focused coverage with portrayals of community leadership and systemic solutions — a standard The Guardian is not consistently meeting within this sample.

The knowledge base further notes that men are quoted approximately three times more often than women in economics and science coverage — a disparity that, combined with The Guardian's limited women-focused story count, suggests that women's authority and expertise may be underrepresented even within articles that nominally address gender. ## 5. Language and Framing

On a positive note, the RSS feed analysis of 30 articles returned **no flagged problematic language or harmful terminology** in headlines. This suggests that The Guardian's editorial standards around surface-level language — avoiding slurs, outdated descriptors, and overtly stigmatising terms — are functioning effectively. However, the absence of harmful terms does not equate to fully inclusive framing. The knowledge base benchmarks highlight that inclusive framing requires people-first language in disability coverage, correct pronoun and name usage for transgender individuals, and avoiding language that implies marginalised identities are exceptional rather than normative. Given that disability coverage was entirely absent from the sample and that LGBTQ+ coverage was limited to a criminalisation story, The Guardian's framing defaults toward crisis and exception rather than normalised, multidimensional representation.

## 6. Community-by-Community Findings

### Women and Gender Equality

Four of the 30 analysed articles addressed women or gender equality, representing 13% of the sample. The most prominent example — *"Men almost twice as likely as women to earn high salaries in 'reality check' on Australia's gender pay gap"* — demonstrates a willingness to cover structural gender inequality with statistical rigour. However, the framing remains primarily problem-focused rather than solution- or leadership-centred. The knowledge base benchmark that women write only 37% of front-page stories across the industry is relevant here: without gender-disaggregated byline data from this sample, it is not possible to confirm whether The Guardian's commissioning is closing or perpetuating that gap. The absence of articles featuring women as political leaders, business authorities, or scientific experts within this 30-article sample is a notable gap.

### LGBTQ+ Communities

Only 2 of the 30 articles — 6.7% of the sample — addressed LGBTQ+ communities, and both appeared within a criminalisation or persecution framing. The article on the two women arrested in Uganda for allegedly kissing in public, and facing a potential life sentence, is an important story to cover; however, it constitutes the entirety of LGBTQ+ representation in the sample. There are no articles featuring LGBTQ+ individuals as experts, community organisers, cultural contributors, or policy voices. This one-dimensional coverage risks reinforcing a narrative in which LGBTQ+ people appear in the news only when they are endangered. The knowledge base notes that LGBTQ+ experts are routinely excluded from mainstream stories — a practice The Guardian's sample does not contradict.

### Racial and Ethnic Minorities

Two articles in the sample addressed racial and ethnic minority communities, representing 6.7% of total coverage. The example cited — *"'More exploitation, fewer rights': Argentina braces for sweeping overhaul of labor laws"* — addresses labour rights in a context with significant implications for migrant and minority workers, though the research findings note this coverage exists within a predominantly negative or precarious framing. The knowledge base benchmark that coverage of Black Americans frequently emphasises crime over community leadership applies as a broader sector concern; within this specific Guardian sample, there is insufficient data to fully assess racial framing beyond the headline level. The limited story count itself is, however, a finding of concern.

### People with Disabilities

**Zero articles** in the 30-article sample addressed disability or featured people with disabilities in any capacity. This represents a total absence of this community from The Guardian's coverage within the audit window. The Media Diversity Watch knowledge base identifies inclusive language and people-first framing as essential benchmarks for disability coverage — but these standards cannot be assessed when the community is entirely invisible in the editorial output. This is the most serious representation gap identified in this audit.

## 8. Areas of Concern and Harm Flags

- **Severe underrepresentation of disability:** With zero articles addressing disability across 30 pieces, people with disabilities are entirely absent from The Guardian's coverage in this audit window — a significant editorial blind spot inconsistent with any claim to inclusive journalism.
- **LGBTQ+ coverage confined to crisis and criminalisation narratives:** Both LGBTQ+-relevant articles in the sample frame the community through persecution, with no counterbalancing coverage of LGBTQ+ leadership, culture, or expertise.
- **Marginalised communities portrayed primarily as victims:** Across women, LGBTQ+, and racial minority coverage, the dominant framing is harm-focused, with limited portrayal of agency, recovery, or authority — contrary to trauma-informed journalism best practice documented in the knowledge base.
- **Sourcing gaps for underrepresented experts:** The research findings indicate an absence of LGBTQ+ experts in non-specific stories and suggest a broader pattern of institutional voices being preferred over community representatives.
- ⚠️ **Complete absence of disability representation constitutes a harm-level visibility gap:** The total exclusion of disability from 30 articles is not a minor underrepresentation — it reflects a systemic editorial failure to treat people with disabilities as a community worthy of routine coverage, which may actively contribute to their social invisibility in public discourse.

## 10. Recommendations

1. **Introduce a mandatory disability coverage quota and beat accountability (Owner: Editorial Director; Timeline: within 3 months).** Given the complete absence of disability coverage in this audit, The Guardian should assign a named disability correspondent or beat editor and establish a minimum monthly story target for disability-related content. Progress should be reviewed quarterly against the Media Diversity Watch benchmark framework.

2. **Expand LGBTQ+ coverage beyond crisis and criminalisation narratives (Owner: Features and Culture Desk Editors; Timeline: within 2 months).** Editors should actively commission stories featuring LGBTQ+ individuals as experts, entrepreneurs, scientists, and community leaders — not only as subjects of persecution. A specific commissioning brief for LGBTQ+ expertise in mainstream topic areas (economics, science, politics) should be developed.

3. **Implement systematic byline gender and diversity tracking (Owner: Managing Editor / Diversity and Inclusion Lead; Timeline: within 6 months).** The Guardian should publish disaggregated byline data — by gender, and where possible by race and disability status — on a quarterly basis. This data should be used to audit beat assignment patterns and address any structural channelling of journalists from marginalised groups into lower-visibility roles.

4. **Adopt trauma-informed and agency-centred framing guidelines across all community coverage (Owner: Standards and Training Editor; Timeline: within 4 months).** Drawing on the Media Diversity Watch knowledge base benchmarks and established trauma-informed journalism frameworks, The Guardian should update its internal style guidance to require that all articles covering marginalised communities include at minimum one community voice with expert or leadership framing, and that purely victimisation-led articles are reviewed at sub-editing stage for contextual balance.
