# Media Inclusivity Knowledge Base

This file contains 18 internal documents designed for use in a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system about media inclusivity.

Each section is one document. In your ingestion script, you can treat each top-level `## Document` heading as a separate document and attach metadata such as:

## Document 1 – Why Inclusive Media Coverage Matters
Angle: multi  
Doc type: guideline  

Inclusive coverage treats marginalized communities as full participants in public life rather than as problems to be solved or objects of curiosity. It recognizes that media narratives can reinforce or challenge inequities in areas such as housing, policing, health, and employment. It also acknowledges that representation is not just about visibility, but about how people are shown, who is allowed to speak, and whose judgment is treated as authoritative. Inclusive journalism therefore looks at staffing, story choices, sourcing, and language as interconnected levers that either uphold or disrupt structural bias.

---

## Document 2 – Definitions and Core Concepts
Angle: multi  
Doc type: guideline  

“Marginalized communities” are groups who have less social, political, or economic power because of systems such as racism, sexism, ableism, homophobia, transphobia, xenophobia, classism, and their intersections. Inclusive coverage requires recognizing that individuals hold multiple identities at once, and that experiences differ within groups. “Tokenization” occurs when one person or story is treated as representing an entire community. “Deficit framing” is a pattern that defines people primarily by what they supposedly lack, instead of by their strengths, expertise, and agency. Recognizing these patterns gives journalists a vocabulary for naming and correcting harmful habits.

## Document 3 – The Four-Angle Evaluation Framework
Angle: multi  
Doc type: guideline  

This framework evaluates coverage through four angles: Bylines and Story Selection, Portrayal Within Content, Sourcing Diversity, and Language and Framing. Bylines and Story Selection examine who gets to decide which stories are told and who receives credit and visibility for that work. Portrayal Within Content focuses on how people are depicted, whether as full human beings with agency and complexity or as flat symbols of a social issue. Sourcing Diversity looks at whose voices are invited in as experts or eyewitnesses and whose knowledge is sidelined. Language and Framing evaluate word choice, headlines, images, and narrative structures that can either humanize or dehumanize communities.

---

## Document 4 – Barriers to Inclusive Bylines and Story Selection
Angle: bylines_story_selection  
Doc type: guideline  

Marginalized journalists often face hiring and promotion barriers, unequal access to high profile beats, and additional emotional labor when covering their own communities. Newsrooms may rely heavily on familiar contributors, which reproduces existing homogeneity in bylines. Editorial pitching systems can unintentionally privilege people with certain educational backgrounds, networking opportunities, or comfort navigating newsroom culture. When stories about marginalized communities are green lit only in moments of crisis or controversy, coverage skews toward trauma and spectacle instead of everyday life and achievement. Recognizing these barriers is the first step toward changing assignment practices.

---

## Document 5 – Checklist for Bylines and Story Selection
Angle: bylines_story_selection  
Doc type: checklist  

Use the following questions when planning coverage.

1. Are people from the community represented among staff, freelancers, and guest contributors on this topic?  
2. Across a month or quarter, how many stories about a community are written by people from that community?  
3. Are marginalized journalists consistently relegated to identity only beats instead of a range of subjects such as politics, tech, environment, or culture?  
4. Do story ideas originate from communities themselves, or only from institutional press releases and officials?  
5. Is the coverage mix balanced between crisis reporting and stories of daily life, joy, creativity, organizing, and leadership?

## Document 6 – Evaluating Portfolio Diversity
Angle: bylines_story_selection  
Doc type: guideline  

A single inclusive story is not enough; patterns across time matter. To evaluate portfolio diversity, chart which communities appear in your coverage over a defined period and in what roles, such as protagonists, experts, background figures, or problems to be solved. Examine who is pictured in photos, featured in headlines, and given follow up stories when issues evolve. Look for repetition: are the same communities appearing only around crime, poverty, or conflict, while others appear in innovation, lifestyle, and success pieces. Use these findings to set concrete targets for expanding beats, commissioning new voices, and revisiting under reported topics from different angles.

## Document 7 – Case Study Template for Bylines and Story Selection
Angle: bylines_story_selection  
Doc type: case_study_template  

For any given piece, use this template.

1. Who pitched the story and what motivated the pitch.  
2. Who holds the byline or bylines; note their relationship, if any, to the community being covered.  
3. Were journalists from the community invited to pitch, co report, edit, or provide cultural insight.  
4. Could this story have been told earlier or differently if newsroom power were more evenly distributed.  
5. How might the story look if someone from the community led the reporting and framing decisions.  

Record answers for multiple stories to reveal systemic patterns, then discuss findings in editorial meetings that focus on structural improvements rather than individual blame.

## Document 8 – Principles for Portrayal Within Content
Angle: portrayal  
Doc type: guideline  

Portrayal should center people’s agency, expertise, and everyday complexity, not just their suffering. Stories should show individuals making decisions, debating ideas, caring for one another, and shaping their environments. Avoid framing people solely as victims, villains, or inspirational exceptions, because these tropes flatten entire communities into one emotional note. When describing challenges such as discrimination or economic hardship, connect them to policies, institutions, and histories rather than implying personal failure. Portrayal that links lived experience to structural context helps audiences understand that inequity is produced, not natural or inevitable.

## Document 9 – Avoiding Stereotypes and Harmful Tropes
Angle: portrayal  
Doc type: guideline  

Certain narratives recur across media, such as the criminal immigrant, the welfare dependent single mother, the ungrateful protester, the tragic disabled person, the model minority, or the predatory queer figure. These tropes simplify complex realities into familiar scripts that justify unequal treatment. To avoid them, ask whether your story relies on stock images, clichés, or sensational anecdotes that do not reflect broader data or community perspectives. Be cautious about using one highly visible individual as the stand in for a diverse group, especially when their experiences are unusual. When you recognize a trope, deliberately seek alternative framings and voices that complicate or overturn it.

---

## Document 10 – Visuals, Headlines, and Portrayal
Angle: portrayal  
Doc type: guideline  

Images and headlines often carry the strongest emotional impact and can either reinforce or counteract bias. Avoid visuals that show marginalized people only in states of distress, surveillance, or passivity when alternatives exist. Ensure that headlines do not reduce communities to problems or erase perpetrators of harm, for example by saying that harm simply happened rather than naming responsible actors. When portraying joy, success, or ordinary life, choose visuals that reflect the diversity of age, body type, disability, gender expression, and other identities within the community. Visual and headline checks should be formal steps in the editing process, not last minute afterthoughts.

## Document 11 – Building Diverse and Trusting Source Networks
Angle: sourcing_diversity  
Doc type: guideline  

Sourcing diversity requires deliberate relationship building beyond official spokespeople and repeat expert voices. Create and maintain a living database of community organizations, grassroots leaders, researchers from underrepresented backgrounds, and everyday residents willing to speak on issues that affect them. Invest time in attending community events, following local outlets run by marginalized groups, and listening to people outside moments of crisis. Clarify how you will use quotes, what context you can provide, and what limitations you have so that sources can make informed decisions about participation. Over time, this approach builds trust and yields richer, more accurate coverage than reliance on a narrow circle of institutional sources.

## Document 12 – Interview Practices with Marginalized Sources
Angle: sourcing_diversity  
Doc type: guideline  

Interviews should minimize extractive dynamics and protect sources from unnecessary risk. Before recording, explain your outlet, the story angle, and potential risks such as online harassment or misinterpretation. Offer options around naming, pronouns, and on the record versus background status, and honor those agreements consistently. Ask open ended questions that invite people to define themselves rather than forcing them into categories you chose. Avoid pressing for traumatic details that are not essential to the story’s purpose, and resist the urge to frame emotion as spectacle. Afterward, consider whether sharing certain quotes or identifiers could lead to harm and, if so, seek additional consent or anonymization.

---

## Document 13 – Metrics and Indicators for Sourcing Diversity
Angle: sourcing_diversity  
Doc type: checklist  

To evaluate sourcing diversity, track whose voices appear in your stories over time. Categorize sources by role, such as expert, official, community member, bystander, person directly harmed, or organizer, and by relevant identity dimensions when they are publicly acknowledged and central to the coverage. Note how often marginalized sources are quoted on technical or policy matters versus only on personal feelings. Look at who is allowed to explain causes and solutions, not only who is described as affected. Use these metrics to set specific newsroom goals, such as increasing the proportion of experts from underrepresented groups in certain beats.

---

## Document 14 – Principles for Inclusive Language
Angle: language_framing  
Doc type: guideline  

Inclusive language avoids slurs, outdated terms, and euphemisms that dehumanize or erase people’s identities. It uses people first or identity first constructions according to the preferences of the communities concerned, recognizing that different groups make different choices. It avoids describing people as illegal, broken, burdensome, or normal versus abnormal, and instead focuses on precise, neutral descriptions of circumstances or policies. It treats gender, race, disability, religion, and other identities as ordinary aspects of human diversity rather than curiosities requiring constant explanation. Style guides and inclusive language resources can support newsroom wide consistency.

## Document 15 – Harmful Frames and How to Replace Them
Angle: language_framing  
Doc type: guideline  

Some frames consistently distort coverage, including law and order frames that treat state violence as neutral enforcement, culture of poverty frames that blame communities for structural deprivation, both sides frames that equate calls for rights with bigoted backlash, and charity frames that position marginalized people only as recipients of benevolence. To replace these, foreground systems, data, and historical context, and show who benefits from the status quo. Highlight community led solutions and organizing rather than portraying change as a gift from powerful institutions. Reframing does not require advocacy for specific policies, but it does require moving beyond simplistic narratives that obscure power and responsibility.

---

## Document 16 – Editing Checklist for Language and Framing
Angle: language_framing  
Doc type: checklist  

Before publishing, editors can run through this checklist.

1. Are any groups named primarily by deficits, labels, or stereotypes rather than by how they self identify.  
2. Do verbs and sentence structures obscure responsibility, for example by saying clashes erupted instead of naming who initiated force.  
3. Are metaphors and analogies accurate and non dehumanizing, avoiding comparisons to disease, contamination, or natural disasters.  
4. Does the story assume a default reader with a particular race, nationality, or class position, or does it speak to a broad audience.  
5. Have you considered whether headlines, captions, and pull quotes might mislead readers about who is harmed and who holds power.

## Document 17 – Newsroom Workflows for Applying the Four Angles
Angle: multi  
Doc type: guideline  

To make the four angle framework routine, integrate it into existing editorial workflows. During pitch meetings, ask explicitly which communities are involved and how the story scores on bylines, portrayal, sourcing, and language. At the draft stage, have editors or peer reviewers complete a short evaluation form noting strengths and risks for each angle. Schedule periodic audits in which a diverse group of staff reviews a sample of recent coverage and identifies recurring gaps or improvements. Use findings to inform training, hiring, partnerships with community media, and updates to style guides, so inclusive decision making becomes part of everyday practice.

## Document 18 – Cross-Angle Quick Reference Guide
Angle: multi  
Doc type: quick_reference  

This quick reference sheet summarizes core questions across all four angles.

Bylines and Story Selection: who pitches, assigns, and writes stories about this community, and are community members in decision making and storytelling roles.  
Portrayal Within Content: are people shown as complex actors with agency, or only as victims, threats, or symbols, and does the story connect individual experiences to structural context.  
Sourcing Diversity: whose knowledge is treated as authoritative, and are community members quoted on analysis and solutions, not just feelings and anecdotes.  
Language and Framing: do wording, headlines, and images respect people’s self descriptions and avoid dehumanizing frames, and does the narrative challenge rather than reproduce stereotypes.  

You can adapt this sheet into a one page form that reporters and editors consult at multiple points in the reporting process, helping to embed inclusivity checks into everyday practice.
